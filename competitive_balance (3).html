The Truth Behind a Salary Cap
Competitive balance

"Baseball has a competitive balance issue"

That is the argument being made to justify a salary cap. But the results don't support it. The game on the field is not broken. The push for a cap is about something else entirely.

Why?

The mechanism

Baseball is the most luck-dependent major sport. Single-game randomness erases regular-season advantages in October.

In baseball, you would need a best-of-75 game series for the better team to advance 80% of the time. In the NBA, you need just a best-of-7. That is how much the structure of the sport itself drives parity in baseball, regardless of payroll.

Best team vs. worst team: single-game win probability
In baseball, the worst team beats the best team 40% of the time in any given game. In basketball, it's 10%.
Better team advances in a best-of-7 series
In the NBA, the better team wins a 7-game series ~80% of the time. In MLB, just ~61%. October is anyone's game.

The bottom line: Small-market teams win more often in baseball than in basketball. More franchises have hoisted the trophy. And the reason is baked into the sport itself. Baseball's game-to-game randomness makes October unpredictable in a way no other playoff format can match. That's why 14 different teams have won the World Series in 20 years, more than any other league. The postseason isn't a coronation. It's a coin flip with a slight edge, and that changes everything. The game is not broken. So why are we being told it is?

So if competitive balance isn't the issue, why push for a cap?

It's about franchise values.

MLB teams generate as much revenue as NBA teams. But they're valued at nearly half the price. The reason: without fixed labor costs, investors discount MLB franchises. This gap has become a fixation for ownership groups, particularly as private equity firms have entered the picture. These aren't traditional sports owners who grew up as fans of the game. They are financial operators, and they see one number that is out of line with every other league: the revenue multiple. A salary cap would fix that number overnight. It has nothing to do with what happens on the field.

Revenue vs. valuation: MLB and NBA teams Same revenue, different values
Each dot is one franchise. At similar revenue levels, NBA teams (blue) are valued billions higher than MLB teams (red). The two clusters barely overlap. That gap is what private equity ownership groups see when they look at their portfolios, and it is the entire reason a salary cap is on the table.

Investors pay 13.4x revenue for an NBA franchise but only 6.1x for MLB. Even the NHL, which generates far less revenue than baseball, commands a higher multiple. The only structural difference? The other three leagues have fixed labor costs. That predictability is what drives valuations, and it is what ownership wants to import into baseball.

The real story: The competitive balance argument is a smokescreen. The data shows baseball's parity is as strong as any league in North American sports and stronger than the NBA, which has a cap. What is actually driving the push for a salary cap is franchise valuations. Private equity firms have entered MLB ownership in recent years, and they have brought with them a focus on valuation multiples that traditional owners never prioritized. Those firms see MLB trading at 6.1x revenue while the NBA trades at 13.4x and the NHL at 8.3x, and they want that gap closed. A salary cap would fix labor costs as a percentage of revenue, give investors the predictability they crave, and push franchise valuations up by billions in aggregate. There is no version of this that benefits players. A cap does not create parity. The results already show parity exists. A cap creates valuation growth for ownership groups. That is the ask. It has been positioned to the public as being about the health of the game. It is about the health of ownership balance sheets. Players and fans should understand the difference.
